Abstract

The rise of AI-driven live impersonation in video conferencing poses a severe threat that current detection methods are ill-equipped to address. Existing deepfake datasets target social media and broadcast content, leaving the conferencing domain critically underserved by reflecting codec compression, constrained head dynamics, and virtual background conditions of platforms like Zoom and Teams. We introduce VCDF-X, the first multimodal video conferencing deepfake dataset and benchmark, comprising over 10,000 videos synthesized by nine state-of-the-art generation pipelines spanning portrait animation (LiveAvatar, SadTalker), face swap (Inswapper + GFPGAN, Hyper-swap + GFPGAN, FaceFusion, VisoMaster), and identity-driven generation (OVI, Dream-ID, LTX-2), with virtual background augmentation to faithfully emulate real-world conferencing conditions.

The defining contribution of VCDF-X is its explainability-first annotation framework: every sample is paired with pixel-level manipulation masks, natural language forgery descriptions, facial action units (FAUs), eye gaze trajectories, depth maps, face mesh and contour geometry, and remote photoplethysmography (rPPG) signals — the richest per-sample annotation set in the deepfake detection literature. Due to the rich modality supplemental materials, one can infer explainable results by building a comprehensive multimodal detector that fuses physiological, geometric, appearance, and semantic cues.

VCDF-X, annotations, and evaluation code will be publicly released to advance robust and interpretable deepfake detection research.

Introduction

Real-time face replacement in video calls is now a practical threat, yet mainstream deepfake benchmarks target broadcast and social media and miss conferencing conditions (codec compression, webcam resolution, head pose, virtual backgrounds), so detectors often fail where it matters most. We present VCDF-X, a multimodal video conferencing deepfake dataset and benchmark built from nine state-of-the-art synthesis pipelines, with rich per-video annotations including forgery masks, natural-language evidence, action units, gaze, depth, mesh, and rPPG for explainable detection.

Dataset

VCDF-X contains 13,768 clips, including 4,151 real and 9,617 fake samples. Real videos were collected from publicly available YouTube content under Creative Commons licenses, focusing on informal, in-the-wild recordings with diverse lighting, camera quality, and subject behavior. The dataset includes heterogeneous resolutions, frame rates, and an average clip length of about 24 seconds.

The dataset is intentionally skewed toward synthetic samples to reflect realistic threat scenarios, with most fakes generated by recent high-capacity models such as LiveAvatar (14B) and LTX-2. It covers common communication settings, including video conferencing calls, one-on-one video chats, and selfie-style recordings.

Facial action units: mean intensity profile

ME-GraphAU estimates frame-level AU intensities; we compare mean intensity of the ten most informative units between strata. Elevated fake response around the mouth and brows (e.g. AU25, AU01, AU26) aligns with speech and expression artifacts common in generative pipelines.

Pairwise coupling between action units

Correlation matrices over the top 15 AUs reveal whether muscle groups co-vary as in real faces. Global structure is similar, but local patches (e.g. AU25–AU26) can differ, suggesting imperfect preservation of natural co-articulation in fakes.

Per-AU intensity densities

Kernel density plots for six representative units show where real and fake mass diverge on the [0,1] intensity scale. Sharp fake peaks at full AU25 intensity and right-shifted AU01 are consistent with the radar summary; near-overlap on AU17 indicates limits of single-AU cues.

Benchmark

We evaluate on FF++, CelebDF, and VCDF-X with an 80/10/10 split and T=32 frames at 224×224. Baselines include ResNet-50, ViT, Swin, MDF (FAU + rPPG fusion), and MMGUnet (RGB + FFT + STFT [+ landmarks]). We report F1 (macro) and AUC for in-domain and cross-dataset settings.

Limitations

Despite its contributions, VCDF-X has limitations: future models may produce unseen artifacts, transmission artifacts are only simulated, and some sub-ethnic groups have limited samples. Future work will target live streams with real-time degradation.
